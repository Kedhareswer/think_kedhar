  "Diagnosis": "Doctors diagnose P. falciparum malaria by combining clinical assessment with laboratory confirmation — treatment should never wait for results in a seriously ill patient.\n- Blood smear microscopy — a drop of blood examined under a microscope; the gold standard for identifying the parasite species and measuring how many red blood cells are infected (parasite density)\n- Rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs) — detect P. falciparum-specific antigens from a finger-prick blood sample within 15–20 minutes; widely used in clinics and field settings where microscopy is unavailable\n- PCR (polymerase chain reaction) — the most sensitive method; used in reference laboratories to confirm species, detect low-level infections, and identify drug-resistance mutations such as Kelch13 variants that signal artemisinin partial resistance\n- Full blood count and metabolic panel — assesses severity by measuring haemoglobin level, blood sugar, kidney function, and lactate\n\nSevere malaria is a clinical emergency: any patient with confirmed P. falciparum infection who shows impaired consciousness, respiratory distress, severe anaemia, or other danger signs must be treated immediately as severe disease — do not wait for parasite density results.",
  "Treatment": "Treatment depends on whether malaria is uncomplicated or severe, and on local resistance surveillance data.\n\nUncomplicated P. falciparum malaria:\n- Artemisinin-based combination therapies (ACTs) are the global first-line treatment.   "Outlook/Prognosis": "Uncomplicated P. falciparum malaria treated promptly with an appropriate ACT carries a very good prognosis — most patients clear parasites within 48–72 hours and make a full recovery.
