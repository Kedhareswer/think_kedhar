**Entity:** ACT overall efficacy maintenance
**Date:** 2026-05-09

### Core Claim

ACT partner drug efficacy protection **requires** ACT overall efficacy maintenance.

*Certainty: High | Evidence grade: Meta-analysis*

---

### Context

- **Regions:** Sub-Saharan Africa, India
- **Setting:** Malaria-endemic, community and clinical
- **Intervention:** Artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT)

---

### Synthesis

ACT regimens pair a fast-acting artemisinin component with a longer-acting partner drug. Resistance to the artemisinin component (partial artemisinin resistance, PfKelch13 mutations) shifts therapeutic burden onto the partner drug. If partner drug efficacy erodes simultaneously — through independent resistance pressure — the combination fails entirely.

This claim formalizes the dependency: **partner drug protection is not freestanding**. It is contingent on the overall ACT system remaining efficacious. In contexts where both components face resistance pressure (documented in parts of sub-Saharan Africa and India per PMID:33556786), erosion of overall ACT efficacy removes the protective redundancy that justifies combination therapy.

**Implication:** Surveillance strategies must track both components jointly. Monitoring artemisinin resistance alone understates true treatment failure risk. Policy decisions on ACT deployment, drug rotation, or combination switching should treat overall ACT efficacy as the primary unit of concern — not component-level efficacy in isolation.

---

### Source

Artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT) and drug resistance molecular markers: A systematic review of clinical studies from two malaria endemic regions — India and sub-Saharan Africa.
[PMID:33556786](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33556786/)

## concepts/hemoglobin-endocytosis.md

## Concept Note: Hemoglobin Endocytosis

**Entity:** `hemoglobin endocytosis`
**Claims:** 2 | **Source:** PMID:32905757 | **As of:** 2026-05-09

---

### Definition

Hemoglobin endocytosis — process by which intraerythrocytic *Plasmodium falciparum* ingests host red blood cell hemoglobin via receptor-mediated or bulk endocytosis into the parasite's digestive vacuole.

---

### Role in Parasite Biology

**P. falciparum* growth requires hemoglobin endocytosis** (certainty: high). Parasite digests ~60–80% of host cell hemoglobin for amino acids and to create space for replication. Block endocytosis → starve parasite, halt growth.

---

### Role in Drug Mechanism

**Artemisinin activation requires hemoglobin endocytosis** (certainty: high). Hemoglobin digestion releases free heme (ferrous iron, Fe²⁺). Fe²⁺ cleaves artemisinin's endoperoxide bridge → reactive radical species → alkylates parasite proteins → parasite death.

**Implication:** Reduced hemoglobin uptake (e.g., in ring-stage parasites or artemisinin-resistant strains with *kelch13* mutations) = less Fe²⁺ = less artemisinin activation = resistance phenotype.

---

### Mechanistic Summary

```
RBC hemoglobin
      │
      ▼ endocytosis (parasite)
 digestive vacuole
      │
      ├─► proteolysis → amino acids → parasite growth
      │
      └─► free heme (Fe²⁺)
                │
                ▼
          artemisinin + Fe²⁺ → radical → parasite death
```

---

### Evidence Quality

| Claim | Certainty | Grade |
|---|---|---|
| Growth requires Hb endocytosis | High | Expert opinion |
| Artemisinin activation requires Hb endocytosis | High | Expert opinion |

Both claims from single source (PMID:32905757). No RCT-level mechanistic data recorded. Status: **pending review**.

---

### Knowledge Gaps

- No quantitative thresholds (what % reduction in endocytosis sufficient to confer resistance?)
- No population/setting qualifiers — unclear if endocytosis rate varies by host immune status or parasite strain
- Evidence grade capped at expert opinion — mechanistic biochemistry studies (e.g., heme quantification assays) not yet linked as supporting claims

---

### Related Entities to Explore

- `artemisinin resistance` — downstream consequence of reduced endocytosis

## Concept Note: Increased Parasite Sensitivity to Piperaquine

**Entity:** Increased parasite sensitivity to piperaquine (PPQ)
**Source:** PMID:40720544 — *"The plasmepsin-piperaquine paradox persists in Plasmodium falciparum"*
**Claims synthesized:** 3 (f7793c02, cac564d0, fc776906)

---

### Summary

Three CRISPR-Cas9 knockout experiments in Cambodian *P. falciparum* isolates converge on one conclusion: **plasmepsin II (PMII) and plasmepsin III (PMIII) copy number amplification drives PPQ resistance**, and their removal restores sensitivity.

| Perturbation | Effect | Comparator |
|---|---|---|
| Double KO (PMII + PMIII) | ↑ PPQ sensitivity | Isogenic multi-copy PMII/III |
| Single KO (PMIII only) | ↑ PPQ sensitivity | Isogenic multi-copy PMIII |
| Single KO (PMII only) | ↑ PPQ sensitivity | Isogenic multi-copy PMII |

Each gene contributes independently; double KO is not required. Both PMII and PMIII alone are sufficient resistance determinants.

---

### Context

- **Setting:** In vitro, endemic Cambodia (PPQ-resistant parasite background)
- **Mechanism implied:** Plasmepsin II/III copy number amplification = canonical PPQ resistance marker in Southeast Asia; knockouts confirm causal link, not mere association
- **Paradox noted in title:** Despite functional validation, clinical/epidemiological data remain discordant — hence "paradox persists"

---

### Evidence Quality

| Dimension | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Certainty | High |
| Evidence grade | Expert opinion (no IC₅₀ values captured in claims) |
| Effect size | Not quantified in extracted claims — fetch source for IC₅₀ data |
| Status | Pending review |

---

### Gaps / Open Questions

1. **Effect size missing** — IC₅₀ fold-change vs. isogenic controls not extracted; needed for clinical relevance
2. **PMII vs. PMIII relative contribution** — single KOs both sensitize, but magnitude comparison absent
3. **Paradox unexplained** — paper title signals tension between in vitro causal evidence and field data; concept note should link to resistance surveillance claims when available
4. **No in vivo / clinical arm** — all in vitro; translational gap unresolved

---

### Synthesis Statement

CRISPR functional genomics confirms PMII and PMIII amplification as **causal, not merely associative**, drivers of PPQ resistance in Cambodian *P. falciparum*. Either gene alone sustains resistance; both must be targeted for full sensitization. Resolving the field paradox requires pairing these in vitro findings with matched clinical IC₅₀ and copy-number data.

Increased copy number of plasmepsin II and III genes confers PPQ resistance vs. single-copy comparators (Cambodia, endemic setting). The resistance association is well-established in Southeast Asia. Notably, southern Angolan isolates show **low prevalence** of pfpm2 CNV — suggesting this mechanism has not yet spread widely in sub-Saharan Africa. The "plasmepsin–piperaquine paradox" (PMID:40720544) persists: plasmepsin CNV alone does not fully account for observed resistance phenotypes, implying co-selection or additional loci.

---

### Mechanism 2 — PfCRT Mutations

**Claims:** `72e10d04`, `69c306c2` | **Certainty:** high (both)

*P. falciparum* carrying chloroquine resistance transporter (CRT/PfCRT) mutations resists PPQ vs. wild-type CRT strains — confirmed in Southeast Asia/Cambodia (PMID:40720544) and in general molecular resistance literature (PMID:32905757). These two claims converge on the same mechanism from independent sources; evidence is concordant. ---

### Claim Concordance & Conflicts

| Pair | Relationship | Note |
|------|-------------|------|
| `72e10d04` ↔ `69c306c2` | **Concordant** | Same mechanism (CRT mutations), two sources; reinforce each other |
| `75d3630a` ↔ `3e09db34` | **Complementary** | Both pfpm2/plasmepsin CNV, different regions; Angola low prevalence tempers generalizability |
| Mech 1 ↔ Mech 2 | **Additive, not resolved** | PMID:40720544 covers both but paradox unresolved — neither mechanism alone sufficient |

### Evidence Gaps

- All 4 claims graded **expert_opinion** despite being sourced from primary molecular epidemiology studies — effect sizes, IC₅₀ values, and ORs absent
- No pediatric, pregnancy, or immunosuppressed sub-population data
- Angola claim (`3e09db34`) should be read as **absence of high-prevalence CNV**, not absence of risk
- Mechanism interaction (CRT + plasmepsin CNV co-occurrence) unstudied in claims

---

### Clinical Implication

PPQ-containing ACTs (DHA-PPQ) face resistance risk primarily in **Cambodia and Southeast Asia** via dual mechanisms. Sub-Saharan Africa currently lower risk for plasmepsin-mediated PPQ resistance but warrants surveillance. CRT mutation monitoring remains essential globally given cross-resistance implications.

**Primary driver: kelch13 (k13) mutations**

In Uganda, PCR-corrected 28-day efficacy upper CI bound approached the WHO 90% threshold in Busia and Arua (ea4fb695, moderate certainty) — borderline, not yet failed, but a warning signal.

## notes/associations/dihydroartemisinin-ic50.md

## Topic Note: associations/dihydroartemisinin-ic50

**Source:** PMID:39545737 | Uganda (eastern + northern, endemic) | *P. falciparum* fresh isolates

---

### Core Findings

DHA IC50 carries two moderate correlations with artemisinin resistance phenotyping assays:

| Association | Spearman rs | Direction |
|---|---|---|
| IC50 ↔ RSA % survival | +0.4235 | Higher IC50 → more parasites survive DHA pulse |
| IC50 ↔ eRRSA fold change | −0.4116 | Higher IC50 → lower extended-recovery fold change |

Both ~0.42 magnitude — moderate, not strong.

---

### Interpretation

RSA % survival positive correlation: expected. Resistant parasites (high IC50) tolerate DHA → survive ring-stage assay.

eRRSA fold change negative correlation: eRRSA measures growth recovery after DHA stress. Resistant parasites show lower fold change — possibly because their baseline survival is already high, compressing the recovery delta.

Two assays converge on same biological signal from different angles. Neither replaces the other fully — shared variance ~17% (rs²).

---

### Caveats

- Evidence grade tagged `expert_opinion` — likely miscoded; data is quantitative correlational, not opinion-based
- No CIs on effect sizes
- Single geographic context (Uganda) limits generalizability

## Topic Note — Etiology: Increased Parasite Sensitivity to Piperaquine (PPQ)

**Source:** PMID:40720544 — *"The plasmepsin-piperaquine paradox persists in Plasmodium falciparum"*

### Mechanism

Plasmepsin II (PMII) and plasmepsin III (PMIII) copy-number amplification is the dominant molecular marker of PPQ resistance in Cambodian *P. falciparum*. | Perturbation | Effect on PPQ sensitivity |
|---|---|
| KO of PMII alone | Increased (↑ sensitivity) |
| KO of PMIII alone | Increased (↑ sensitivity) |
| Double KO (PMII + PMIII) | Increased (↑ sensitivity) |

---

### Key Inferences

- **Both paralogs contribute independently** — single KO of either gene suffices to shift sensitivity, so resistance is not solely PMIII- or PMII-driven.
- **Additive/redundant architecture** — double KO doesn't necessarily imply synergy; each copy independently confers resistance.

- **"Paradox" framing** — title signals ongoing debate: PPQ binds heme in digestive vacuole; plasmepsins are DV aspartyl proteases. Mechanistic link between protease copy number and PPQ binding/efflux still unresolved.

All three claims graded `evidence_grade: expert_opinion` despite being in-vitro CRISPR experiments — likely a tagging artifact. Experimental manipulation with isogenic controls is mechanistic bench evidence, stronger than opinion. Flag for regrading to `in_vitro_experimental`.

## 1. Core Resistance Architecture

### 1.1 Artemisinin Resistance — Two Convergent Mechanisms

Both pathways terminate at the same node: **reduced free heme → less ART activation → parasite ring-stage survival**.

```
PATHWAY A — Kelch13 (primary, high certainty)
K13 mutation (C580Y, R539T, Y493H, R561H, A675V, C469Y)
  → ↓ K13-mediated ubiquitination of PfPI3K
  → ↑ PfPI3K stability → ↑ PI3P
  → ↑ autophagy / UPR (ER stress response)
  → enhanced ring-stage proteostatic buffering
  → + ART-induced ER stress → UPR → further autophagy upregulation
  → parasite survives DHA pulse

PATHWAY B — Pfcoronin (secondary, moderate certainty)
Pfcoronin mutation (G50E, R100K, E107V)
  → impaired PfCoronin–PfActin interaction
  → PfCoronin loses ring-stage localization
  → impaired endocytosis
  → ↓ hemoglobin acquisition → ↓ free heme (Fe²⁺)
  → less ART endoperoxide bridge cleavage
  → reduced drug activation → resistance

CONVERGENCE POINT:
Both K13 (via hemoglobin endocytosis disruption) and Pfcoronin (via actin/endocytosis)
limit heme substrate. K13 additionally operates via PI3P/autophagy axis.
Convergence claim: low certainty — needs co-culture / genetic interaction studies.
```

**Key mechanistic dependencies:**
- Hemoglobin endocytosis required for both parasite growth AND artemisinin activation (PMID:32905757, high certainty)
- K13 C580Y → elevated PI3P → upregulated autophagy → MRT68921 (autophagy inhibitor) shows *lower* IC50 in resistant vs WT (synthetic lethality opportunity; in vitro only, no IC50 values)
- ART itself induces ER stress → UPR → autophagy upregulation; C580Y parasites exploit this as survival buffer

---

### 1.2 Resistance Amplifiers / Companion Mutations

Layer on top of primary K13 resistance:

| Mutation | Gene | Effect | Certainty | Evidence |
|----------|------|--------|-----------|---------|
| arps10-V127M | Apicoplast ribosomal protein S10 | Further ↑ RSA₀₋₃ₕ vs K13-C580Y alone | Moderate (low for GWAS) | Expert opinion |
| fd-D193Y | Ferredoxin | Additive with arps10-V127M on C580Y background | Moderate | Expert opinion |
| PfATG18 T38I | Autophagy-related protein 18 | Co-occurs with Y493H, R539T, C580Y in SEA field isolates | Moderate | Expert opinion |
| A212T (K13 second site) | Kelch13 | Amplifies OZ277 + OZ439 resistance on C580Y and R539T backgrounds | Moderate–High | Expert opinion |

**Critical pattern:** A212T is a **generalizable resistance amplifier** — it potentiates multiple primary K13 alleles (C580Y, R539T) against both conventional artemisinins AND next-gen ozonides (OZ277, OZ439). This is the most dangerous next-gen resistance signal currently in the evidence base.

### 1.3 Metabolic Reprogramming (Co-occurring, Causal Direction Unclear)

In resistant vs sensitive isolates (meta-analytic, moderate certainty):
- Pentose phosphate pathway downregulated
- Glycolytic pathway downregulated
- Ring-stage fatty acid metabolism ART-insensitive in C580Y mutants (multi-omics, PMID:37385107)

Interpretation: reduced glucose/ROS flux may lower hemoglobin digestion and ART activation — consistent with "quiescence" model. **Cause vs. consequence unresolved.**

## 3. ACT Treatment Failure — Integrated Picture

ACT failure is **polygenic and regimen-specific**:

```
ACT failure = artemisinin resistance component + partner drug resistance component

For AL:    pfmdr1 86Y/184F/1246Y + (K13 or pfcoronin)
For AS+SP: pfdhps 437G + pfdhfr 51I/59R/108N (triple mutant) + ART resistance
For DHA-PPQ: pfpm2/3 CNV + PfCRT mutations + K13

Pfcrt 76T: pan-ACT failure signal, not regimen-specific (moderate certainty)
```

**ACT partner drug protection dependency**: Partner drug efficacy is not freestanding — it is contingent on overall ACT system efficacy. Surveillance must track both components jointly. Monitoring ART resistance alone understates true failure risk.
