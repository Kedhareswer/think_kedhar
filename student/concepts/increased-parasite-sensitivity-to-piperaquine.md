## Concept Note: Increased Parasite Sensitivity to Piperaquine

**Entity:** Increased parasite sensitivity to piperaquine (PPQ)
**Source:** PMID:40720544 — *"The plasmepsin-piperaquine paradox persists in Plasmodium falciparum"*
**Claims synthesized:** 3 (f7793c02, cac564d0, fc776906)

---

### Summary

Three CRISPR-Cas9 knockout experiments in Cambodian *P. falciparum* isolates converge on one conclusion: **plasmepsin II (PMII) and plasmepsin III (PMIII) copy number amplification drives PPQ resistance**, and their removal restores sensitivity.

| Perturbation | Effect | Comparator |
|---|---|---|
| Double KO (PMII + PMIII) | ↑ PPQ sensitivity | Isogenic multi-copy PMII/III |
| Single KO (PMIII only) | ↑ PPQ sensitivity | Isogenic multi-copy PMIII |
| Single KO (PMII only) | ↑ PPQ sensitivity | Isogenic multi-copy PMII |

Each gene contributes independently; double KO is not required. Both PMII and PMIII alone are sufficient resistance determinants.

---

### Context

- **Setting:** In vitro, endemic Cambodia (PPQ-resistant parasite background)
- **Mechanism implied:** Plasmepsin II/III copy number amplification = canonical PPQ resistance marker in Southeast Asia; knockouts confirm causal link, not mere association
- **Paradox noted in title:** Despite functional validation, clinical/epidemiological data remain discordant — hence "paradox persists"

### Synthesis Statement

CRISPR functional genomics confirms PMII and PMIII amplification as **causal, not merely associative**, drivers of PPQ resistance in Cambodian *P. falciparum*. Either gene alone sustains resistance; both must be targeted for full sensitization. Resolving the field paradox requires pairing these in vitro findings with matched clinical IC₅₀ and copy-number data.
