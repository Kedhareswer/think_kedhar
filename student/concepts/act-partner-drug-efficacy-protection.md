## Concept Note: ACT Partner Drug Efficacy Protection

**Entity:** ACT partner drug efficacy protection
**Date:** 2026-05-09
**Evidence grade:** Meta-analysis (systematic review)
**Certainty:** High

---

### Core Claim

Partner drug efficacy protection **requires** maintenance of overall ACT efficacy. Without preserved partner drug activity, artemisinin-based combination therapy loses its combinatorial defense against *Plasmodium* resistance selection.

---

### Context

| Dimension | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Regions | Sub-Saharan Africa, India |
| Setting | Malaria endemic |
| Therapy | Artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT) |
| Population | Not age/pregnancy/immune-status restricted |

---

### Mechanism Logic

ACTs pair a fast-acting artemisinin component (clears bulk parasitemia) with a slower partner drug (clears residual parasites). Partner drug efficacy is the **last line of combination defense**: if the partner drug fails, artemisinin-partial resistance + partner drug resistance = full treatment failure.

---

### Evidence Base

Systematic review of clinical studies across two high-burden endemic regions (sub-Saharan Africa + India). Molecular resistance markers tracked alongside clinical outcomes. Source: [PMID:33556786](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33556786/).

---

### Implications

- Resistance surveillance must track **partner drug markers** (e.g., *pfkelch13*, lumefantrine/amodiaquine resistance loci) — not artemisinin partial resistance alone
- Policy: ACT regimen switching decisions depend on partner drug efficacy data, not just artemisinin component data
- Regional heterogeneity (Africa vs. India) means blanket ACT policy carries risk — local resistance profiles govern efficacy
