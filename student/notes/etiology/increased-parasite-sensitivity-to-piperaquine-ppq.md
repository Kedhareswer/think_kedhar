## Topic Note — Etiology: Increased Parasite Sensitivity to Piperaquine (PPQ)

**Source:** PMID:40720544 — *"The plasmepsin-piperaquine paradox persists in Plasmodium falciparum"*

---

### Mechanism

Plasmepsin II (PMII) and plasmepsin III (PMIII) copy-number amplification is the dominant molecular marker of PPQ resistance in Cambodian *P. falciparum*. CRISPR-Cas9 knockout experiments confirm the causal direction: removing these copies **restores PPQ sensitivity** in isogenic Cambodian parasite backgrounds.

| Perturbation | Effect on PPQ sensitivity |
|---|---|
| KO of PMII alone | Increased (↑ sensitivity) |
| KO of PMIII alone | Increased (↑ sensitivity) |
| Double KO (PMII + PMIII) | Increased (↑ sensitivity) |

All three interventions tested against isogenic controls retaining multiple PMII/PMIII copies (the resistant baseline).

---

### Key Inferences

- **Both paralogs contribute independently** — single KO of either gene suffices to shift sensitivity, so resistance is not solely PMIII- or PMII-driven.
- **Additive/redundant architecture** — double KO doesn't necessarily imply synergy; each copy independently confers resistance.
- **Causal, not correlative** — CRISPR isogenic design rules out linkage-drag confounds; amplification *is* the mechanism, not a bystander marker.
- **"Paradox" framing** — title signals ongoing debate: PPQ binds heme in digestive vacuole; plasmepsins are DV aspartyl proteases. Mechanistic link between protease copy number and PPQ binding/efflux still unresolved.
